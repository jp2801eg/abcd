## Facilitator Opening Remarks

Denise opened at 6:07 PM, thanked Father Ignacio and the parish for hosting, and acknowledged that the building has held neighborhood gatherings for over 40 years. She explained the purpose of the session:

> "Tonight we're not here to talk about what's broken. We know what's broken. Tonight we're mapping what's already here — the skills, the relationships, the places, the things that make this neighborhood alive — so we have a real foundation to build from."

Marcus distributed large sticky-note pads and markers. Denise introduced the four guiding questions written on butcher paper hung at the front:

1. What can you do that others might not know about?
2. Who do people around here go to when they need something?
3. What places matter in this neighborhood?
4. What do you love about Clark-Fulton that you'd want to protect?

She emphasized: *"There are no wrong answers. If it matters to you, it belongs on the wall."*

---

## Section 1 — Warm-Up: "Gifts in the Room"

Denise asked participants to write one skill or knowledge they personally bring to the neighborhood — anonymous, posted to the "What We Know" board.

**Selected responses (transcribed from sticky notes):**

- "I speak English, Spanish, and a little Arabic — came in handy during COVID"
- "Plumbing. I've fixed sinks in half this block."
- "I bake. I bring food to anyone who just had a baby or lost someone."
- "I know this neighborhood's history back to the 1960s. I remember when Fulton Road was all Czech businesses."
- "I run a youth flag football league — unpaid"
- "Nursing. Night shifts at MetroHealth but I'm here on my off days."
- "I fix cars. I don't advertise but my neighbors know."
- "I've been sober 11 years and I help people find their way in."

Denise noted the range: *"You just told me this room has a plumber, a nurse, an interpreter, a food provider, a mechanic, a historian, and a recovery mentor. That's not nothing. That's a neighborhood."*

**[FACILITATOR NOTE — Marcus]:** Group energy shifted noticeably here. Mrs. Cunningham (longtime resident, 70s) said, "I never thought of what I do as an asset." Several people nodded. This moment may be worth returning to in future sessions.

## Section 2 — Associations: "Who Organizes Around Here?"

Open discussion. Denise asked: *"What groups, clubs, circles — formal or informal — are already active in Clark-Fulton? These don't have to be nonprofits. A group of moms who share rides counts."*

**Identified associations (composite from discussion and cards):**

| Group / Network | Description | Key Contact Named |
|---|---|---|
| Clark-Fulton Moms Network | Informal WhatsApp group, ~85 members, mostly Spanish-speaking; shares school info, ride coordination, safety alerts | Rosa |
| St. Michael Parish Men's Group | Weekly Thursday meetings; runs food pantry 2x/month | Father Ignacio |
| Fulton Rd. Block Club (3600 block) | Monthly; active since 2003; handles alley maintenance, tree planting | Mrs. Cunningham |
| Clark Ave. Merchants Circle | Informal association of ~12 storefront owners; meets quarterly at El Rincon | Sal |
| Cleveland Youth Boxing Club | Rents space at the rec center; 30–40 youth participants | DeShawn |
| Recovery community (informal) | Described as "word of mouth" — residents in recovery who connect newcomers to AA, NA, and housing | Not named; "everyone knows who to call" |
| Intergenerational Knitting Circle | Meets at the library branch; 8–12 members; multigenerational, mixed background | Bridget |
| Czech & Slovak Heritage Club (legacy) | Noted as less active; older generation; annual picnic at Denison Park still draws ~60 people | Mr. Prochazka |

Rosa raised her hand to note that the Moms Network is not on any official list: *"We're not a nonprofit, we don't have a website. But we know things about this neighborhood faster than anyone."* General agreement in the room.

Terrell asked whether the boxing club should be listed under "youth programs" rather than associations. Denise said the distinction doesn't matter tonight — *"We can sort it later. Right now we just want to see it."*

## Section 3 — Institutions: "Who Has Resources and Relationships?"

Denise introduced this section by distinguishing between associations (driven by people who *choose* to be there) and institutions (organizations that have a formal presence and resources). *"Both matter. But they're different kinds of assets."*

**Institutions identified:**

**Anchor institutions:**
- **MetroHealth System** (main campus, W. 25th St.) — Multiple residents work there; also cited as primary healthcare provider for uninsured community members. Luz noted that the new campus construction brought some local hiring. *[ACTION: Confirm MetroHealth community health worker program — Jerome said his neighbor is one]*
- **Cleveland Metropolitan School District** — Lincoln-West HS and Orchard STEM School both named. Darla, a paraprofessional at Orchard, said the principal has been trying to build community connections but "doesn't always know who to call."
- **Cleveland Public Library — South Branch** (Fulton & Denison) — Named multiple times. Bridget: "That library is doing more for this neighborhood than people realize. Immigration help, ESL, job applications — all of it."

**Faith institutions:**
- La Sagrada Familia (hosting tonight's session)
- St. Michael the Archangel Parish
- New Hope Baptist Church (Antoine: "They run a summer program, free lunch every day, 200 kids last year")
- Luz named a Pentecostal church on Clark she could not recall the name of — noted as important to the newer Central American community

**Public/government:**
- Cleveland Ward 14 Council office — mixed responses; Yolanda said they've been responsive on street lighting; Terrell said "only during election years." Both noted.
- Cuyahoga County JFS — named as a resource, but with reservations ("hard to navigate," "not always welcoming," multiple voices)
- Cleveland EMS Station 21 on Fulton — Tamara noted strong relationships between the crew and longtime residents; "they know people by name"

**[FACILITATOR NOTE — Marcus]:** Some tension emerged during the institutions section. A few participants expressed frustration that institutions often come into the neighborhood without building real relationships. Denise held the space well — acknowledged the frustration, named it as important data, and brought the group back to identifying what's there before evaluating it.

## Section 4 — Built & Natural Environment: "What Places Shape This Neighborhood?"

Denise unrolled a large printed street map of Clark-Fulton and placed it on the center table. Participants were handed colored dot stickers: green = "asset/strength," yellow = "potential/underused," red = "concern."

**Conversation highlights by location:**

**Parks and green space:**
- **Denison Park** — received the most green dots. Jerome: "That park kept me out of trouble. It keeps kids out of trouble now." Basketball courts cited as daily social hub in summer. Concern about lighting after dark (two red dots on the east path).
- **Brookside Park / Metropark (nearby)** — mentioned as "close but feels disconnected." Kenji: "It's beautiful but people from this neighborhood don't feel like it's for them." *[Important equity note — flagged for follow-up]*
- Community garden on W. 41st (behind Orchard school) — Yolanda manages it; 18 plots; waitlist of 12 households. Named as one of the most requested assets to expand.

**Commercial / built environment:**
- Fulton Road corridor — mixed response. Long-time residents miss Czech/Slovak storefronts; newer residents value the taquerias, carnicerías, and quinceañera shops. Mrs. Prochazka: "I miss what it was." Rosa, gently: "But it feeds my family now." Both received nods. Denise let that sit.
- El Rincon Restaurant — named multiple times as a gathering place and informal community hub
- The old Czech Cultural Garden building on W. 38th — vacant for 3 years; yellow dot. Multiple people said "someone should do something with that."
- W. 25th Street Development (MetroHealth corridor) — visible from the neighborhood; opinions divided on whether it's helping or displacing

**Transportation:**
- RTA Route 22 (Clark Ave.) — named as critical; concern about recent schedule cuts
- Walking conditions on Fulton near the I-90 underpass — red dots; described as unsafe at night

## Section 5 — Economic Assets: "What's the Economic Life of This Neighborhood?"

Denise: *"When money circulates in a neighborhood — when people spend locally, hire locally, invest locally — that's an asset. What does that look like here?"*

**Local businesses and economy:**
- **Sal's Auto Glass** (Clark Ave.) — Sal noted he employs 4 local residents, all from the neighborhood. Has resisted offers to sell the building: "I've been here 22 years. This is my place."
- Several small restaurants along Fulton — Rosa said the carnicería owner (unnamed, not present) employs teenagers for after-school delivery work
- Home-based businesses mentioned: catering (Yolanda), hair braiding (Tamara), tax preparation (unnamed resident, "very trusted," word of mouth only)
- Jerome mentioned a guy two blocks over who does small engine repair out of his garage and "never advertises but always busy"

**Land and property:**
- Several vacant lots identified on the map — one on W. 47th that neighbors have informally maintained for 5+ years; Bridget: "We mow it, we planted flowers, we treat it like ours. But it's not ours."
- Mrs. Cunningham noted that 3 homes on her block are now rental-owned by an out-of-town LLC. "I don't know who to call when something breaks."

**Workforce and skills as economic assets:**
- Multiple participants noted that neighborhood residents work at MetroHealth, the schools, and local businesses — but often don't know each other in that capacity. Kenji: "We're all part of the same economy but we don't connect the dots."
- Darla raised the idea of a neighborhood skills exchange or time bank — well-received; no one knew if anything like that existed in Cleveland.

## Section 6 — Cultural Assets: "What Makes This Neighborhood, This Neighborhood?"

This section ran long (nearly 40 minutes) — highest energy of the evening.

Denise asked: *"What are the stories, traditions, flavors, sounds, and histories that belong here and nowhere else?"*

**What participants named:**

**History and memory:**
- Mr. Prochazka spoke at length about the Czech and Slovak families who built the parish, the butcher shops, the sokol (gymnastic) halls. "My grandfather built two of those buildings with his hands. That's not gone — it's in the walls."
- Mrs. Cunningham recalled the neighborhood's history as a destination for Black families during the Great Migration: "My family came from Mississippi in 1951. This block was our foothold." *[Both of these accounts exist in the same neighborhood — both were received with respect]*
- Luz described how Central American families, many from Guatemala and Honduras, began arriving in the 1990s and 2000s: "We made this neighborhood ours, too. The food, the church, the quinceañeras — that's culture, that's history."

**Celebrations and traditions:**
- Annual Czech/Slovak Heritage Picnic at Denison Park — Mr. Prochazka; ~60 attendees, homemade kolaches, polka music
- Quinceañera season on Fulton — Rosa described it as "like a neighborhood festival, every Saturday in spring and summer. The dresses, the limos, the families — the whole block celebrates."
- New Hope Baptist's Juneteenth Block Party — Antoine: "We've been doing it for 19 years. Last year, 400 people came."
- Informal Día de los Muertos altar at La Sagrada Familia — described as increasingly attended by non-Latino neighbors: "People come who didn't grow up with it. They bring photos of their people too."

**Arts and expression:**
- Mural on the side of El Rincon (painted 2019 by a local artist, name not recalled) — multiple people mentioned it; described as showing neighborhood history across cultures
- DeShawn mentioned that a few young men from the boxing club have started doing spoken word at a coffee shop on W. 25th — "it started as something to do after sparring. Now they perform."
- Hana noted that oral history is particularly important in the immigrant community — "people carry whole histories in their memory. They just don't get asked."

**[FACILITATOR NOTE — Denise]:** At one point Terrell said, "I didn't know about the Czech picnic. I've lived two blocks from that park for eight years." Mr. Prochazka said, "You're invited." That moment felt significant — write it up for the final report.

## Closing and Next Steps

Denise summarized the session's output: *"You just mapped a neighborhood. You showed us individuals with extraordinary skills. You showed us groups that are already organizing. You showed us institutions with resources. You showed us places that matter. You showed us economic life that's local and real. And you showed us a culture — actually, multiple cultures — that are living and present. This is your foundation."*

She asked for a show of hands: who would be willing to be part of a follow-up "connector team" to help link these assets to each other? Eleven hands went up.
